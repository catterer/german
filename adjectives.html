German Adjective Declension: Definite and Indefinite Articles

This page provides a comprehensive overview of adjective endings with **definite articles** (der, die, das) and **indefinite articles** (ein, eine) in all four cases. Each card shows an example, the correct ending, and a mnemonic to aid memorization.

Definite Articles (der, die, das)

Nominative (Who or What?)

The nominative is the basic case of the subject. Adjective endings here are often "weak" because the definite article does most of the work.

Masculine (der)

der alt-e Mann

Mnemonic: "EASY E" – The article "der" is strong and indicates gender and case, allowing the adjective to take the easy way out.

Feminine (die)

die alt-e Frau

Mnemonic: "EASY E" – The article "die" is strong and does the work, allowing the adjective to take the easy way out.

Neuter (das)

das alt-e Kind

Mnemonic: "EASY E" – The article "das" is strong and does the work, allowing the adjective to take the easy way out.

Plural (die)

die alt-en Leute

Mnemonic: "EN-DURING N" – All plural adjective endings with an article are "-en". It's the most common ending, so it endures!

Accusative (Whom or What?)

The accusative is the case of the direct object. Pay attention to the change in the masculine gender.

Masculine (den)

den alt-en Mann

Mnemonic: "EN-DURING N" – The masculine accusative is the "N-case" in German, so "-en" fits well.

Feminine (die)

die alt-e Frau

Mnemonic: "EASY E" – Feminine remains the same in accusative as in nominative, so it's easy.

Neuter (das)

das alt-e Kind

Mnemonic: "EASY E" – Neuter remains the same in accusative as in nominative, so it's easy.

Plural (die)

die alt-en Leute

Mnemonic: "EN-DURING N" – Plural adjectives always take "-en" in the accusative.

Dative (To Whom or What?)

The dative is the case of the indirect object. Here, adjective endings are consistent for all genders and the plural.

Masculine (dem)

dem alt-en Mann

Mnemonic: "EN-DURING N" – In the dative, "-en" is the predominant ending for all genders.

Feminine (der)

der alt-en Frau

Mnemonic: "EN-DURING N" – In the dative, "-en" is the predominant ending for all genders.

Neuter (dem)

dem alt-en Kind

Mnemonic: "EN-DURING N" – In the dative, "-en" is the predominant ending for all genders.

Plural (den)

den alt-en Leuten

Mnemonic: "EN-DURING N" – Plural adjectives always take "-en" in the dative.

Genitive (Whose?)

The genitive indicates possession or belonging. Here too, adjective endings are consistent for most genders and the plural.

Masculine (des)

des alt-en Mannes

Mnemonic: "EN-DURING N" – In the genitive, "-en" is the predominant ending for all genders.

Feminine (der)

der alt-en Frau

Mnemonic: "EN-DURING N" – In the genitive, "-en" is the predominant ending for all genders.

Neuter (des)

des alt-en Kindes

Mnemonic: "EN-DURING N" – In the genitive, "-en" is the predominant ending for all genders.

Plural (der)

der alt-en Leute

Mnemonic: "EN-DURING N" – Plural adjectives always take "-en" in the genitive.

Indefinite Articles (ein, eine)

This section shows adjective endings with **indefinite articles** (ein, eine). Note that the indefinite article does not exist in the plural; here, the strong declension (without an article) is used.

Nominative (Who or What?)

In the nominative with indefinite articles, adjective endings often indicate the gender when the article does not.

Masculine (ein)

ein alt-er Mann

Mnemonic: "STRONG R" – The article "ein" does not indicate the gender here, so the adjective must be strong and take "-er".

Feminine (eine)

eine alt-e Frau

Mnemonic: "EASY E" – The article "eine" indicates the gender, so the adjective can take it easy.

Neuter (ein)

ein alt-es Kind

Mnemonic: "ESS-ENTIAL S" – The article "ein" does not indicate the gender here, so the adjective must be strong and take "-es".

Plural (No Article)

alt-e Leute

Mnemonic: "EASY E" – Without an article in the plural (nominative/accusative), the ending is often "-e".

Accusative (Whom or What?)

The accusative with indefinite articles. Here too, the masculine case requires special attention.

Masculine (einen)

einen alt-en Mann

Mnemonic: "EN-DURING N" – Masculine accusative is always "-en", regardless of whether it's a definite or indefinite article.

Feminine (eine)

eine alt-e Frau

Mnemonic: "EASY E" – Feminine remains the same in accusative as in nominative, so it's easy.

Neuter (ein)

ein alt-es Kind

Mnemonic: "ESS-ENTIAL S" – Neuter remains the same in accusative as in nominative, so it's easy.

Plural (No Article)

alt-e Leute

Mnemonic: "EASY E" – Without an article in the plural (nominative/accusative), the ending is often "-e".

Dative (To Whom or What?)

In the dative with indefinite articles, adjectives almost always take the "-en" ending.

Masculine (einem)

einem alt-en Mann

Mnemonic: "EN-DURING N" – "-en" is the standard ending in the dative after indefinite articles.

Feminine (einer)

einer alt-en Frau

Mnemonic: "EN-DURING N" – "-en" is the standard ending in the dative after indefinite articles.

Neuter (einem)

einem alt-en Kind

Mnemonic: "EN-DURING N" – "-en" is the standard ending in the dative after indefinite articles.

Plural (No Article)

alt-en Leuten

Mnemonic: "EN-DURING N" – In the dative plural, the ending is always "-en", even without an article.

Genitive (Whose?)

The genitive with indefinite articles. Adjectives here mostly take the "-en" ending.

Masculine (eines)

eines alt-en Mannes

Mnemonic: "EN-DURING N" – "-en" is the standard ending in the genitive after indefinite articles.

Feminine (einer)

einer alt-en Frau

Mnemonic: "EN-DURING N" – "-en" is the standard ending in the genitive after indefinite articles.

Neuter (eines)

eines alt-en Kindes

Mnemonic: "EN-DURING N" – "-en" is the standard ending in the genitive after indefinite articles.

Plural (No Article)

alt-er Leute

Mnemonic: "STRONG R" – In the genitive plural without an article, the ending is "-er".
